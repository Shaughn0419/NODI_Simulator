## Core Computation Logic Explained (Expanded Edition)

*Physics, Formulas, Design Rationale, and Literature Basis for Each Step*

## 1. Architecture Overview

### 1.1 The Problem We Are Solving

When a nanoparticle (e.g., a $20\text{--}100\,\mathrm{nm}$ gold nanoparticle or exosome) flows through a sub-micrometer nanochannel on a fluidic chip, the scattered light generated by probe laser illumination is extremely weak. For a $40\,\mathrm{nm}$ gold nanoparticle, the scattering cross section is on the order of $10^{-15}\,\mathrm{m}^2$ — far below the direct detection limit of conventional optical systems.

The NODI (Nanofluidic Optical Diffraction Interferometry) system addresses this by not measuring the weak scattering intensity directly. Instead, it uses the diffracted light from the nanochannel itself as a reference field, which interferes with the particle scattering field, thereby linearly amplifying the weak signal to a detectable level. This principle is closely related to how iSCAT microscopy uses coverslip reflection as a reference.

This simulator does not aim to predict absolute signal values at specific experimental points. Its goal is to systematically compare the relative detectability across different combinations of particle type, laser wavelength, channel width, and channel depth, thereby providing parameter space screening for experimental design.

### 1.2 Why a Layered Design

If all physical processes were combined in a single function, two problems arise: first, it becomes difficult to localize errors to a specific layer; second, modifying one physical model would propagate changes throughout the entire system. The layered design ensures each layer has independent physical meaning and auditable intermediate quantities.

For example, modifying the noise model does not require recalculating Mie scattering; changing particle material does not require reconfiguring peak extraction; adjusting scoring weights does not require re-running interference calculations. This modular maintainability is essential for large-scale parameter sweeps.

This layered approach is standard in the interferometric scattering microscopy (iSCAT) community. The canonical signal model decomposes the detection process into: particle scattering, reference field generation, interferometric superposition, and detector readout.

- [1] Taylor, R.W. & Sandoghdar, V. Nano Lett. 19, 4827-4835 (2019). — Basic iSCAT signal model
- [2] Tsuyama, Y. & Mawatari, K. Anal. Chem. 96, 11430-11438 (2024). — Experimental basis of NODI
- [3] Tsuyama, Y. & Mawatari, K. Microfluid. Nanofluid. 26, 63 (2022). — NODI detection principle
### 1.3 Seven-Layer Computation Chain

The complete chain consists of seven layers, where the output of each layer feeds into the next:

- **Layer 1: Intrinsic Particle Scattering (Mie Theory)**
  Question answered: How strongly does this particle scatter at a given wavelength and medium? What is the angular distribution and complex phase of the scattered field?

- **Layer 2: Detection Angle and Angular Collection**
  Question answered: Over what angular range does the experimental system collect scattered light? How do the finite aperture, slit, and pinhole affect collection efficiency?

- **Layer 3: Reference Field Model**
  Question answered: How strong is the diffracted reference field from the nanochannel? How does it depend on channel geometry and wavelength?

- **Layer 4: Particle Trajectory and Illumination**
  Question answered: How does the particle move through the channel? How strongly is it illuminated at each position? What phase does the beam itself carry?

- **Layer 5: Interferometric Detection Signal**
  Question answered: After superposition of reference and scattered fields, what time-domain signal does the detector see?

- **Layer 6: Readout and Noise**
  Question answered: After lock-in amplifier readout and various noise sources, what is the final observable signal?

- **Layer 7: Peak Extraction and Engineering Scoring**
  Question answered: Can this signal be detected? Over many random events, what are the detection rate, stability, and $\mathrm{SNR}$? How should different designs be ranked?

## 2. Layer 1: Intrinsic Particle Scattering (Mie Theory)

### 2.1 What This Layer Solves

Given a spherical particle, we need to know its scattering properties at a given wavelength and medium. This includes three aspects: the total amount of scattered light (cross section); the angular distribution of scattered intensity; and the complex amplitude phase at each angle (material scattering phase).

This layer deliberately answers only questions about the particle itself, without involving channel geometry, reference field, noise, or other system factors. The physical rationale is that a particle's intrinsic optical properties are determined by its material, size, and surrounding medium, and should not depend on the detection system configuration.

For spherical particles, this problem has an exact analytical solution — Mie theory, proposed by Gustav Mie in 1908. It is the rigorous solution of Maxwell's equations under spherically symmetric boundary conditions.

### 2.2 Input Parameters and Their Physical Meaning

- **Particle radius** $a$

Directly controls the size parameter $x = \frac{2\pi n_m a}{\lambda}$, which is the primary determinant of Mie scattering strength. In the Rayleigh limit ($x \ll 1$), the scattering cross section scales as $a^6$ — meaning halving the particle diameter reduces scattering intensity by a factor of 64. This is the fundamental reason why small particle detection is so challenging, and why interferometric amplification methods like NODI are necessary.

- **Complex refractive index** $n_p(\lambda) = n_{\mathrm{real}} + i\,n_{\mathrm{imag}}$

The real part determines scattering strength; the imaginary part determines absorption. For gold nanoparticles, the complex refractive index varies dramatically with wavelength, with both scattering and absorption strongly enhanced near the localized surface plasmon resonance (around $520\,\mathrm{nm}$). This is not a simple $1/\lambda^4$ trend but a dispersion relation determined by the material's electronic structure. The simulator uses experimentally measured tabulated data from Johnson & Christy (1972) with interpolation, rather than constant approximations.

- **Medium refractive index** $n_m$

Approximately 1.33 for water in the visible range. The ratio $m_{\mathrm{rel}} = n_p / n_m$ is the core input to Mie calculations. When the particle refractive index is close to the medium (as for biological particles in water), scattering is extremely weak — such particles are called optically soft.

- [4] Bohren, C.F. & Huffman, D.R. Absorption and Scattering of Light by Small Particles. Wiley (1983), Ch. 4.
- [5] Johnson, P.B. & Christy, R.W. Phys. Rev. B 6, 4370-4379 (1972).
- [6] Mie, G. Ann. Phys. 25, 377-445 (1908).
### 2.3 Mie Coefficient Computation

The Mie scattering coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$ are the weights for each multipole order, corresponding to electric and magnetic multipole contributions respectively. Their computation requires Riccati-Bessel functions (psi_n and xi_n) and their derivatives.

The numerical implementation follows Bohren & Huffman (1983): first, the Wiscombe (1980) criterion determines the series truncation order $n_{\max} = x + 4x^{1/3} + 2$; then downward recursion computes the logarithmic derivative $D_n(mx)$ (much more numerically stable than upward recursion); upward recursion computes $\psi_n(x)$ and $\xi_n(x)$; finally, boundary condition formulas yield $a_n$ and $b_n$.

This algorithm has been extensively validated over the past four decades and produces reliable results for x ranging from 0.01 to several thousand.

- [7] Wiscombe, W.J. Appl. Opt. 19, 1505-1509 (1980).
### 2.4 Cross Sections and Efficiency Factors

$$
Q_{\mathrm{sca}} = \frac{2}{x^2}\sum_n (2n+1)\left(|a_n|^2 + |b_n|^2\right)
$$

$$
Q_{\mathrm{ext}} = \frac{2}{x^2}\sum_n (2n+1)\,\mathrm{Re}(a_n + b_n)
$$

$$
C_{\mathrm{sca}} = Q_{\mathrm{sca}}\,\pi a^2
$$

$$
C_{\mathrm{abs}} = C_{\mathrm{ext}} - C_{\mathrm{sca}}
$$

Q values exceeding 1 indicate that the particle's effective scattering cross section exceeds its geometric cross section — common under resonance conditions, reflecting the particle's ability to collect incident field over an area larger than its physical size.

### 2.5 Angular Amplitude Functions

Mie theory provides the complex amplitude of scattered light at any angle through the angular amplitude functions $S_1(\$\theta$)$ and $S_2(\$\theta$)$:

$$
S_1(\theta) = \sum_n \frac{2n+1}{n(n+1)} \left[a_n \pi_n(\cos\theta) + b_n \tau_n(\cos\theta)\right]
$$

$$
S_2(\theta) = \sum_n \frac{2n+1}{n(n+1)} \left[a_n \tau_n(\cos\theta) + b_n \pi_n(\cos\theta)\right]
$$

S1 corresponds to the perpendicular polarization component; S2 to the parallel component. Both are complex-valued — the argument carries phase information and the modulus determines directional scattering strength.

The unpolarized-average differential cross section:

$$
\frac{dC_{\mathrm{sca}}}{d\Omega} = \frac{|S_1|^2 + |S_2|^2}{2k_m^2}
$$

An important note on dimensions: $\sqrt{dC_{\mathrm{sca}}/d\Omega}$ has units of length (meters) and physically corresponds to the scale of the scattering amplitude. It is not an absolute electric field value but an intrinsic quantity proportional to the scattered field amplitude at a given detection angle. The square root arises because the differential cross section is proportional to the square of the scattering amplitude (Bohren & Huffman, 1983, Eq. 4.61), making $\sqrt{dC/d\Omega}$ a natural field-amplitude proxy suitable for comparison with the reference field in the downstream interference model.

### 2.6 Why Output Complex Field Amplitude, Not Just Cross Section

This is a critical design choice. Conventional scattering detection (dark-field microscopy) only needs the scattering cross section or intensity. However, interferometric scattering detection requires the complex electric field — both amplitude (signal strength) and phase (interference condition).

In the phase-aware main chain, we retain $S_2/k_m$ as a full complex quantity (parallel projection mode). Its modulus gives scattering strength at a given angle; its argument $\arg(S_2/k_m)$ gives the material scattering phase $\$\phi$_{\mathrm{material}}$.

For gold nanoparticles, this phase undergoes a near-$\pi$ jump around the plasmon resonance (about $520\,\mathrm{nm}$). This means the same particle at different wavelengths may produce positive pulses (constructive interference) or negative pulses (destructive interference). Discarding this phase information would make the model unable to explain why detection rate suddenly drops at certain wavelength-particle combinations.

- [8] Young, G. & Kukura, P. Annu. Rev. Phys. Chem. 70, 301-322 (2019).
- [9] Priest, L. et al. Chem. Rev. 121, 11937-11970 (2021).
## 3. Layer 2: Detection Angle and Angular Collection

### 3.1 Why This Layer Is Needed

Mie theory outputs the complete angular distribution over the full $4\pi$ steradian solid angle. The experimental system, however, collects only a fraction of this. In Tsuyama's experiment, the collection side uses an $\mathrm{NA}=0.9$ objective lens, plus a $1\,\mathrm{mm}$ mechanical slit and a $400\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ pinhole. These optical elements define a specific angular collection window.

If a particle's scattering is concentrated outside this window (e.g., a large particle's forward scattering cone is very narrow), even a large total scattering cross section may not result in effective detection. Therefore, we must extract the portion of the angular distribution that the system actually collects.

- [2] Tsuyama & Mawatari (2024), Fig. 2
### 3.2 Channel Diffraction Angle Model

In the NODI system, the detection angle is not arbitrary but determined by the channel's own diffraction geometry:

$$
\theta_{\mathrm{base}} = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{m\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}}{W}\right), \qquad \lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{\lambda_0}{n_m}
$$

This relationship comes directly from classical diffraction theory. As W decreases toward $\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}$, the diffraction angle increases, spreading more light over wider angles.

Failure boundary: when $m\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} \ge W$, the arcsin argument reaches or exceeds 1, and the formula becomes physically undefined. This corresponds to channel widths approaching or below the effective wavelength in the medium, where the channel no longer behaves as a classical diffracting aperture but more like a sub-wavelength waveguide. In such cases, the system automatically falls back to a fixed-angle mode or calibration table lookup, and records this fallback in diagnostics.

- [10] Goodman, J.W. Introduction to Fourier Optics, 4th ed. (2017), Ch. 4.
### 3.3 Finite-Angle Collection Operator

$$
E_{\mathrm{sca,det}} = \iint L_{\mathrm{det}}(\theta,\phi)\,E_{\mathrm{sca}}(\theta,\phi)\,d\Omega
$$

The operator $L_{\mathrm{det}}$ combines: a Gaussian angular kernel in $\theta$ (finite angular bandwidth); a pupil apodization function (high-angle rolloff); a slit function in $\phi$ (spatial filtering by the mechanical slit); a pinhole function (further angular restriction); and a polarization projection (only the component co-polarized with the reference field interferes effectively).

- [11] Hecht, E. Optics, 5th ed. Pearson (2017), Ch. 10.
### 3.4 Normalization

$$
E_{\mathrm{sca,norm}} = \frac{E_{\mathrm{sca,det}}}{E_{\mathrm{sca,ref}}}
$$

All cases are normalized to the same baseline particle under the same detection operator. Critical constraint: the baseline must use exactly the same operator configuration as the runtime case. This absorbs all unknown absolute proportionality constants and retains only relative variations between designs.

## 4. Layer 3: Reference Field Model

### 4.1 Physical Role of the Reference Field

The core amplification mechanism in NODI is interference. In the weak scattering limit, the detection signal is approximately $2|E_{\mathrm{ref}}||E_{\mathrm{sca}}|\cos(\Delta\$\phi$)$. A weak scattering field is linearly amplified by the strong reference field.

Consider a concrete example: if $|E_{\mathrm{ref}}| = 100|E_{\mathrm{sca}}|$, then the self-scattering term $|E_{\mathrm{sca}}|^2 = 1$ (relative units) while the interference term $2|E_{\mathrm{ref}}||E_{\mathrm{sca}}| = 200$. The interference signal is 200 times stronger than self-scattering. This is why NODI can detect single $20\,\mathrm{nm}$ gold nanoparticles.

In classical iSCAT, the reference field comes from Fresnel reflection at the coverslip (reflectivity about 0.4%). In NODI, it comes from diffraction of the probe beam by the nanochannel itself.

- [1] Taylor & Sandoghdar (2019).
- [3] Tsuyama & Mawatari (2022).
### 4.2 Depth Phase Grating Model

The nanochannel acts as a water-filled rectangular groove embedded in a glass substrate. The refractive index difference between water (n=1.33) and glass (n=1.46) creates a phase delay:

$$
\delta_{\mathrm{ref}} = \frac{2\pi |\Delta n| H}{\lambda}
$$

According to thin phase grating theory (Goodman, 2017, Ch. 4), the first-order diffraction field amplitude is proportional to:

$$
E_{\mathrm{ref}} \propto 2\left|\sin\!\left(\frac{\delta_{\mathrm{ref}}}{2}\right)\right|
$$

Key physical implications: (1) For small $\delta$ (shallow channel or long wavelength), the $\sin$ approximates linearly. (2) Maximum diffraction occurs at $\delta = \pi$ (i.e., $|\Delta n|H = \lambda/2$). (3) For $\delta$ exceeding $\pi$, oscillatory Raman-Nath behavior emerges.

For Tsuyama's parameters (H=$550\,\mathrm{nm}$, $\Delta n = 0.13$, $\lambda = 660\,\mathrm{nm}$): $\delta = 0.68\,\mathrm{rad}$, giving $2|\sin(0.34)| = 0.67$.

- [10] Goodman (2017), Ch. 4.
- [11] Hecht (2017), Ch. 10.
### 4.3 Angular Spectrum Structure

The rectangular cross-section produces a sinc-type angular envelope. The full reference field is integrated through the same detection operator as the scattered field:

$$
E_{\mathrm{ref}} \propto \iint L_{\mathrm{det}}\,\mathrm{sinc}\!\left(\frac{Wk\sin\theta\cos\phi}{2\pi}\right)\,2\left|\sin\!\left(\frac{\delta}{2}\right)\right|e^{i\phi_{\mathrm{ref}}}\,d\Omega
$$

A critical design choice: $E_{\mathrm{ref}}$ and $E_{\mathrm{sca}}$ must pass through the same detection operator $L_{\mathrm{det}}$. This ensures physical consistency of the interference term.

### 4.4 Physical Constraints on Reference Field Strength

The reference field strength parameter $\rho$ is not a free empirical parameter but is bounded by physics. From phase grating theory, the first-order diffraction efficiency is $\eta_1 = \sin^2(\delta/2)$, with corresponding amplitude $|\sin(\delta/2)|$. The system exports a physically motivated range ($\rho_{\mathrm{physical\_envelope\_lower/upper}}$) and checks whether the actual $\rho$ falls within it.

## 5. Layer 4: Particle Trajectory and Illumination

### 5.1 Particle Motion in the Channel

**Deterministic Flow**

Particles move along y with a parabolic velocity profile (rectangular channel Poiseuille flow). Tsuyama (2024) reports typical flow velocity of $0.2\,\mathrm{mm}/\mathrm{s}$ at $100\,\mathrm{kPa}$, with particles traversing the detection region in about $10\,\mathrm{ms}$.

**Brownian Diffusion**

Particles undergo random walks in x and z directions. The diffusion coefficient follows the Stokes-Einstein relation:

$$
D = \frac{k_B T}{6\pi \eta a}
$$

For a $40\,\mathrm{nm}$ gold nanoparticle in water at $25^\circ\mathrm{C}$, $D$ is approximately $1.1\times10^{-11}\,\mathrm{m}^2/\mathrm{s}$. In $10\,\mathrm{ms}$ $transit\ time$, the average diffusion length $\sqrt{2D \cdot 10\,\mathrm{ms}}$ is about $470\,\mathrm{nm}$ — comparable to the channel dimensions ($500\text{--}2000\,\mathrm{nm}$), indicating that diffusion significantly affects particle position during detection.

- [12] Einstein, A. Ann. Phys. 17, 549-560 (1905).
### 5.2 Near-Wall Hindered Diffusion

Near channel walls, hydrodynamic interactions suppress diffusion. The physical reason: fluid must be squeezed through the narrow gap between particle and wall, increasing drag.

$$
D_{\mathrm{local}} = D\,f(a/h)
$$

where h is the particle center-to-wall distance, $\chi = a/h$. The parallel correction is approximately $f = 1 - \frac{9\chi}{16} + \frac{\chi^3}{8} - \cdots$ ; perpendicular suppression is stronger.

- [13] Happel, J. & Brenner, H. Low Reynolds Number Hydrodynamics. Springer (1983).
- [14] Deen, W.M. AIChE J. 33, 1409-1425 (1987).
### 5.3 Gaussian Beam Illumination

$$
A_{\mathrm{env}}(t) = \exp\!\left[-\frac{(x-x_f)^2}{w_x^2} - \frac{(y-y_f)^2}{w_y^2} - \frac{(z-z_f)^2}{w_z^2}\right]
$$

### 5.4 Beam Phase: Gouy Phase and Wavefront Curvature

$$
\phi_{\mathrm{Gouy}}(y) = \arctan\!\left(\frac{y-y_f}{z_R}\right)
$$

where the wavefront radius of curvature $R_{\mathrm{eff}}$(y) has the standard closed-form expression:

$R_{\mathrm{eff}}$(y) = (y - y_f) + z_R^2 / (y - y_f) = (y - y_f) [1 + (z_R / (y - y_f))^2]

This is a standard result from Gaussian beam theory (Kogelnik & Li, 1966; Saleh & Teich, 2019, Ch. 3). For small defocus |y - y_f|, $R_{\mathrm{eff}}$ diverges (planar wavefront) and $\$\phi$_{\mathrm{curv}}$ vanishes; for large defocus >> z_R, $R_{\mathrm{eff}}$ approaches (y - y_f) (spherical wavefront). Therefore:

$$
\phi_{\mathrm{curv}}(x,z,y) = \frac{0.5\,k_m\left((x-x_f)^2 + (z-z_f)^2\right)(y-y_f)}{(y-y_f)^2 + z_R^2}
$$

At the focal plane ($y = y_f$), the numerator vanishes and $\$\phi$_{\mathrm{curv}}$ is strictly zero. Only off-focus, off-axis particles experience the curvature phase.

- [23] Kogelnik, H. & Li, T. Appl. Opt. 5, 1550-1567 (1966). — Standard derivation of Gaussian beam parameters
- [15] Saleh, B.E.A. & Teich, M.C. Fundamentals of Photonics, 3rd ed. Wiley (2019), Ch. 3.
## 6. Layer 5: Interferometric Detection Signal

### 6.1 Core Formulas

$$
E_{\mathrm{det}}(t) = E_{\mathrm{ref}}(t) + E_{\mathrm{sca}}(t)
$$

$$
\mathrm{signal}(t) = |E_{\mathrm{det}}(t)|^2 - |E_{\mathrm{ref}}(t)|^2 = |E_{\mathrm{sca}}|^2 + 2\,\mathrm{Re}\!\left(E_{\mathrm{ref}}E_{\mathrm{sca}}^\ast\right)
$$

The first term (self-scattering) scales as $a^6$; the second (interference) scales as $a^3$. For nanoparticles, the interference term dominates by 2-3 orders of magnitude.

### 6.2 Weak Scattering Limit

$$
\mathrm{signal} \approx 2|E_{\mathrm{ref}}||E_{\mathrm{sca}}|\cos(\Delta\phi)
$$

Under shot-noise-limited detection, the $\mathrm{SNR}$ scales with $|E_{\mathrm{sca}}|$ (not $|E_{\mathrm{sca}}|^2$), so halving the scattering amplitude reduces $\mathrm{SNR}$ by only $2\times$, not $4\times$.

- [1] Taylor & Sandoghdar (2019).
### 6.3 Sources of Differential Phase

1. **Material scattering phase** $\phi_{\mathrm{material}}$

From $\arg(S_2/k_m)$. For gold, undergoes a near-$\pi$ jump around the plasmon resonance.

2. **Path phase** $\phi_{\mathrm{path}}$

$$
\phi_{\mathrm{path}} \approx k_m z \cos(\theta_{\mathrm{det}})
$$

Primary cause of position-dependent signal polarity variations ($phase\ flips$).

3. **Beam phase** $\phi_{\mathrm{beam}}$

Gouy phase (arctan, total jump $\pi$) and wavefront curvature phase.

4. **Reference phase** $\phi_{\mathrm{ref}}$

From the channel diffraction structure, including the depth phase grating contribution and $\pi$ jumps at sinc zeros.

- [16] Mahmoodabadi, R.G. et al. Opt. Express 28, 25969-25988 (2020).
### 6.4 Overlap Factor

When $E_{\mathrm{ref}}$ and $E_{\mathrm{sca}}$ have different angular distributions (which they do — channel diffraction and Mie scattering produce different patterns), the interference efficiency is reduced. The $overlap\ factor$ quantifies this mismatch. Values near 1 validate the separated-integration approximation; values significantly below 1 indicate the need for joint angular integration.

## 7. Layer 6: Readout and Noise

### 7.1 Dual-Channel Lock-in Amplifier Model

In Tsuyama (2024), POD (photothermal absorption) and NODI (interferometric scattering) signals share a single photodetector and are separated by a dual-channel lock-in amplifier (LI5660, NF Corporation):

Channel 1 (POD): extracts the component synchronized with the excitation laser modulation frequency (e.g., $4.1\,\mathrm{kHz}$).

Channel 2 (NODI): extracts the high-frequency component not synchronized with modulation (e.g., $1.2\,\mathrm{kHz}$).

Tsuyama showed that at low modulation frequencies ($0.5$-$1.2\,\mathrm{kHz}$), NODI signal leaks into the POD channel. Only above $4.1\,\mathrm{kHz}$ does POD purity exceed $80\%$.

- [2] Tsuyama & Mawatari (2024), Fig. 4.
### 7.2 Noise Model

**Pre-readout noise**

Gaussian noise (detector/amplifier thermal noise), shot noise proportional to reference field intensity, and linear drift:

$$
\mathrm{shot\_noise}(t) \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\ \mathrm{shot\_noise\_scale}^2\,I_{\mathrm{proxy}}(t)\right)
$$

This captures a fundamental limit of interferometric detection: stronger reference fields provide greater amplification but also more shot noise. The optimal reference strength balances these two effects.

**Post-readout noise**

Additional noise and baseline offset after the lock-in amplifier.

- [17] Kukura, P. et al. Nat. Methods 6, 923-927 (2009).
## 8. Layer 7: Peak Extraction and Engineering Scoring

### 8.1 Threshold Estimation

$$
\mathrm{robust\_std} = 1.4826 \cdot \mathrm{MAD}(\mathrm{signal}_{\mathrm{bg}})
$$

$$
\mathrm{threshold} = \mathrm{median}(\mathrm{signal}_{\mathrm{bg}}) + N_\sigma \cdot \mathrm{robust\_std}
$$

MAD is chosen over standard deviation for robustness against outliers. The coefficient 1.4826 makes MAD consistent with the standard deviation for normal distributions.

- [18] Huber, P.J. Robust Statistics. Wiley (1981).
### 8.2 Peak Detection Modes

positive mode: Detects only positive pulses. For simplified scenarios without phase models.

absolute mode (default): Detects peaks on $|\mathrm{signal}|$ but records original sign and polarity. Essential when position-dependent phase models can produce negative pulses — otherwise valid physical signals would be misclassified as undetected.

### 8.3 Three-Tier Scoring System

$\mathrm{score}$ (exploratory): peak height + detection rate - CV

$\mathrm{engineering\_score}$: adds stable detection rate, $\mathrm{threshold}$ margin, local $\mathrm{SNR}$, $\mathrm{ROC\!-\!AUC}$, $d^\prime$, $phase\ flip$ penalty

$\mathrm{final\_engineering\_score}$: hard gates first (minimum detected events, Wilson lower bound on detection rate, stable rate, $phase\ flip$ upper bound, peak margin), then ranked by $\mathrm{engineering\_score}$ within the passing set

The Wilson small-sample correction provides conservative confidence bounds when event counts are low, preventing overestimation of detection rates from too few events.

- [19] Wilson, E.B. J. Am. Stat. Assoc. 22, 209-212 (1927).
- [20] Green, D.M. & Swets, J.A. Signal Detection Theory and Psychophysics. Wiley (1966).
